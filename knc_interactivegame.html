Game 1 — Alert Simulator

Train the real skills KNC observers use the moment a gravitational wave alert fires. Every scenario is based on a real event.

1

📡 Read the Alert

A GCN circular arrives — just like the real ones KNC members receive. It tells you what type of event was detected, where in the sky it came from, how certain the position is, and how urgent the follow-up window is. A skymap shows the localization region.

2

🌤 Check Your Conditions

Your observatory's current seeing, moon phase, target altitude, and sky transparency are shown. Good conditions let you go deep. Poor conditions — or a target below the horizon — may mean sitting this one out.

3

🔭 Observe or Skip?

Weigh everything up and make your call. Not every alert is worth chasing — a retracted event or a stormy night are completely valid reasons to pass. Experienced observers know when to say no.

4

✓ See the Outcome

Find out what the right call was and exactly why. The feedback explains the science behind each decision.

💡 Skymap tip: A long banana arc = only 2 LIGO detectors fired → big uncertain region to search. A compact blob = all 3 detectors triangulated well → much smaller, efficient search. Retracted events show a red ✕ — always skip those!
Skymap shapes you'll see
🍌 GW Banana
Long arc across the sky. 2-detector LIGO event. Wide search area — requires many observers.
🫧 GW Blob
Compact island(s). 3-detector event. Well-constrained — focused search possible.
🎯 Swift GRB
Precise bullseye. Swift-XRT narrows position to arcminutes — you can point directly.
✕ Retracted
Red cross. False alarm — the event was withdrawn. Do not observe.

Game 2 — Transient Classifier

You're doing what the KNC machine-learning pipeline does — but with your eyes and brain. This is exactly the skill our NASA-funded ML project is built on.

1

🖼 Look at the Image Triplet

You see three images side by side: Science (new photo), Reference (old archived photo of the same spot), and Difference (Science minus Reference). A real new source shows up in Science, is absent in Reference, and leaves a clean round blob in Difference. Artifacts look messy — streaks, double blobs, or sharp single pixels.

2

📈 Study the Lightcurve

The lightcurve shows how bright the source is over days. Each shape is a fingerprint — kilonovae fade in days, supernovae take weeks to months, GRB afterglows decay as smooth power-laws. The reference chart below shows what each one looks like.

3

✓✗ Real or Bogus?

Is this a real astrophysical source, or an image artifact? Check the Difference image morphology and the metadata (especially PSF FWHM vs. seeing — a source sharper than the atmosphere allows is always bogus).

4

🔬 If Real — What Type?

Classify using lightcurve shape, fading speed, host galaxy offset, and colour. Remember: you never see GRBs directly — only their optical afterglows. Short GRB afterglows (compact mergers) may carry a kilonova; long GRB afterglows (collapsing stars) do not.

Lightcurve shape reference — what each transient looks like
💛 Kilonova
Peaks in <1 day. Fades fast. Turns red quickly (r-process lanthanides).
⚡ Short GRB afterglow
Power-law decay. Blue. May show a KN red bump at ~2 days.
💥 Long GRB afterglow
Power-law decay, steepens at jet break (~2d). No KN bump. Blue.
🌟 Type Ia Supernova
Slow ~15d rise. Secondary i-band bump at +30d. Weeks–months.
🌀 Core-collapse SN
Long plateau ~80d (Type II-P). Star-forming host. Very slow.
🕳 Tidal Disruption
Smooth rise ~40d. t⁻⁵/³ decline. Nuclear. UV-bright.
🔥 AGN Flare
Slow, irregular variability. Source visible in reference. Nuclear.
✗ Bogus
Single-epoch spike. No real lightcurve. FWHM anomaly.
💡 Key rule: A source with FWHM smaller than the atmospheric seeing is always a detector artifact — the atmosphere blurs everything to at least the seeing size. No real star or transient can beat the atmosphere.
